THE OIL AND NATURAL GAS INDUSTRY

Oil and natural gas have long been the backbone of the global energy sector, powering industries and shaping economies worldwide. Despite growing advancements in renewable energy, these fossil fuels remain crucial due to their versatility and high energy density.

Today, oil and natural gas continue to fuel transportation, manufacturing, and energy production, even as the world shifts toward a more sustainable future.

Hence why oil prices have a significant impact on the global economy. Around the world, it influences everything from transportation costs to inflation rates, i.e., when prices rise, consumers face higher fuel costs, which can increase the cost of goods and services across industries.

Conversely, falling oil prices can lead to economic relief but may also negatively affect oil-exporting countries. Fluctuations in oil prices are driven by factors such as geopolitical tensions, production levels, and global demand, making them a key indicator of economic stability and growth.

Oil Reserves Per Capita

The term oil reserves refers to an estimate of the amount of crude oil located in a particular economic region with the potential of being extracted. Crude oil can be referred to as petroleum.
Crude oil is a naturally occurring fossil fuel - meaning it comes from the remains of dead organisms, and

is made up of a mixture of hydrocarbons - hydrogen and carbon atoms, in liquid form in underground reservoirs within sedimentary rock. Alternatively, it can be found near the surface in oil sands, hence, their overwhelming presence in the Middle-East. However, Venezuela, a country in South America, stands alone as the country

with the largest oil reserve in the world, ahead of second place, Saudi Arabia.
